Reflections

Reflection 1 / 11 September 2025

Photographs hold captive both our minds and reality itself. As Susan Sontag states in “On Photography,” “to collect photographs is to collect the world.” When we take photographs, we are given the chance to simultaneously capture what is real while imbuing our own unique consciousness. This constant presence of truth is what isolates photography from other art forms and makes it so fascinating.


When working creatively, photography offers itself as a basis to influence other artistic pursuits. A photograph is often a reference point. Real imagery guides the process of representing truth through illustration. It aids us in reproducing accurately things that we really should remember, but cannot always due to the limits of our visual memories. Photography adds an element of accessibility to visual art that was not always present. You can create anything from anywhere in a technological age that makes us privy to images all the time. The mind cannot be trusted to illustrate all the gory details of a moment which it has only once bore witness to, but a photograph can. This helps the visual artist grasp reality more firmly. Or alternatively, for the opposite to occur. To be subversive, we first have to know the authenticity of what we are subverting against. Sontag expresses that taking a photo is “as simple as turning the ignition key or pulling the trigger.” The bias we concurrently inject into images is what makes the use of a camera both dangerous and at times, beautiful. We know what we know through the hearts of others.


When we create film, one image becomes hundreds becomes tens of thousands of photos, reflecting both narrative and documentary scenarios. Sontag states that, “photographs may be more memorable than moving images, because they are a neat slice of time, not a flow” and simultaneously expresses that said film images are often “underselected.” This observation is apt and Sontag proves her point well. While still photography may, more often than not, possess more poignancy than film, we can only hope that a skilled and intentional filmmaker is able to grasp some of this poignancy and elevate it through their own medium. I personally find our manmade ability to capture the “flow” of time like this to be a powerful vehicle. It is a capability to expand an isolated precious moment into a series of them that we can better understand humanity and ourselves through. It offers a plethora of even more opportunities to expose difficult truths, tell beautiful stories, and manipulate what we know as reality in unique, enthralling, and grotesque ways. About photographs, Sontag writes that “they are attempts to contact or lay claim to another reality.” I think that motion pictures offer an equal if not at times more compelling attempt to do so. Regardless, before the cameraperson presses record, they point their camera towards a single image.


Photography is a weapon of reality, unreality, growth, and stagnation. It is distant yet at times, unbearably close to us. How we wield this weapon is personal to each individual user.



Reflection 2 / 16 October 2025

The rise of AI and subsequent AI slop is frightening to me on a fundamental level. This year, I made a conscious decision to stop using most social media platforms and to start living a more offline life. AI slop factored into this decision for me. Despite this, I have never been anti-technology and I have always had a love for the digital world. Throughout my life, the internet has been a safe space for me: a place where I could see my own humanity easily reflected back on myself, despite all of its dangers and misgivings. I trusted the online world in a way that I can’t anymore. As Nesrine Malik states in the Guardian article “With ‘AI Slop’ distorting our reality, the world is sleepwalking into disaster,” “You are immersed deeper and deeper into subjective worlds rather than objective reality.” We as humans have trained machines to think and create on our behalves in very sinister ways.


Generative AI images are inherently dangerous and political. Even if it is an image of something innocuous, it is poisonous because nothing about it is real. A human had no part in it, sans training the machine to have such capabilities. We are thus accustoming ourselves to see humanity where it is not. If anything, it is these innocuous images that are the scariest. I myself have seen images of people, even, that were so detailed I either had no idea it was AI or I felt very uncertain. If AI is already this skilled, could we be looking at a future where human art and machine art are entirely indistinguishable?


This is why, in my opinion, creating art right now is more important than ever, not just to combat the culture, but for our personal well-being. There is a radical sense of agency in creation when we now live in a world that requires it less and less.


When the power of AI injects itself into blatantly political situations, it has the power to alter consciousness and morality in a significant way. Malik writes that “Generative AI is trained on pre-existing data, which research has shown is inherently biased against ethnic diversity, progressive gender roles and sexual orientations, therefore concentrating those norms in the output.” It is a political brainwashing tactic of unheard of proportions, because it has massive reach and preys on our human sense of trust in a way that we do not yet have adequate practice discerning against. For people of the older generation, who already understandably have trouble evaluating online content, the significance of this is tenfold. Fascist ideals are propagating the online ecosystem, redefining societal norms, and planting seeds of conservatism in the minds of vulnerable people. Meanwhile, important social concerns are lost more and more in the clutter of it all, as Malik says, “The sense of urgency and action that our crisis-torn world should inspire is […] blunted by how information is presented.”


I fear the long-term effects of this new information economy and mourn the digital landscape of the past. In the face of it all, we must challenge ourselves to be human.



Reflection 3 / 30 October 2025

In Tetteh’s story, ‘My Punishment’ from Al Jazeera’s “Graphic Novel: Motherhood in crisis,” the use of illustration allows for a much more intimate and emotionally poignant depiction compared to what traditional photography or film might allow for. There were multiple instances that stood out to me in her story where I felt like I could really see what her inner world must have felt like. It allowed me to sympathize with her in a unique way.


Tetteh is the victim of very serious abuse at the hands of her husband and the visuals detailed this. When the author writes, “I thought my husband had hurt my baby. He kicked me in my stomach. It felt like a chain around my waist,” paired with a drawing of Tetteh lying on the ground in the fetal position clutching at her stomach, I felt a feeling of dread in my own stomach and it brought the metaphor to life in a chilling way.


Again we see Tetteh’s perspective of her husband in the scene depicting how, “[her] husband struggled to find work. He began to drink, gamble, and steal the money [she] made from selling fish and rice in the street.” Accompanying this is a drawing of the husband portrayed as a complete silhouette. He has no face, physicality, or distinguishable features apart from existing as a looming frame. This is how she sees him: he is a monstrous entity in her life. This is a meaningful example of how illustrated storytelling allows for symbolism in a way that traditional photographs cannot. Sometimes, in order to convey an accurate feeling of something, a picture portraying “reality” is unable to tell the whole story. The truth held within photographs cannot always contain that of the mind’s eye. The perspective of an individual person is colored with experiences, biases, and mentalities that are often wrapped up in webs and webs of nuance and complexity. Illustration is a spectacular means of bringing this to life.


Another great example of this is the slide depicting Tetteh with her husband's ghost in the background. Alongside the drawing she says, “I kept seeing my dead husband's ghost. I was shaking and afraid. I felt my body was shutting down.” The realism of photography does not allow for ghosts. While his ghost might not be physically real, this manifestation of her dead husband is very real within the scope of Tetteh’s consciousness.


As well, using illustration allows the artist to portray contrast in a unique way. Two instances of this stood out to me. First, the artist draws the husband when they first get married accompanied by the text, “I was happy to marry him.” The next slide depicts a more sinister portrayal of her husband, paired with “but soon things changed.” I think that if the story was purely written word, these lines would surely be impactful but it might be easy to glaze over them. However, with the addition of the illustration, with the background color even changing from a subtle pink to a harsh orange, we can feel the violence of the shift.


The next instance of striking contrast which stood out to me was the slide stating, “I have been at the prison for three years and three months. My son -- Alie, is two years and eight months old. He stayed with me throughout. My smart, funny boy.” then subsequently, “They took him from me in September 2019. He is now at the Don Bosco children’s shelter. I’ve only seen him once since he left.” We see Alie drawn playing in the vicinity of Tetteh and then -- nothing. He is gone. The illustrations and their slide-to-slide narrative allow this feeling of emptiness to hang for a moment.


Illustration is able to encapsulate a certain quality of the human experience that text and photographs are not always able to. In the case of Tetteh’s story, it made it feel all the more real and meaningful.
